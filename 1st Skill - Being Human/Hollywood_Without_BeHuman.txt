Hollywood is a neighborhood in Los Angeles, California, that serves as the historical center of the American film industry. The film industry began moving to Hollywood in the early 1910s, fleeing Thomas Edison's patent restrictions on the East Coast. By the 1920s, Hollywood had become the dominant force in global cinema, producing thousands of films each year.

The Hollywood Walk of Fame, established in 1958, features over 2,700 stars embedded along 15 blocks of Hollywood Boulevard and three blocks of Vine Street. Each star commemorates a notable figure in film, television, music, radio, or theater. The TCL Chinese Theatre, opened in 1927, is another major landmark, known for its famous forecourt filled with celebrity handprints, footprints, and autographs.

The Academy Awards, commonly known as the Oscars, have been presented annually in Hollywood since 1929. The ceremony honors outstanding achievements in filmmaking and is broadcast to over 200 countries worldwide. The Dolby Theatre has hosted the event since 2002.

Hollywood experienced significant decline in the late 20th century as film production moved to other locations and the neighborhood faced economic challenges. However, revitalization efforts in the 2000s brought new businesses, restaurants, and theaters to the area.

The Hollywood Sign, originally erected in 1923 as a real estate advertisement reading "Hollywoodland," stands at 450 feet tall on Mount Lee. It remains one of the most iconic landmarks in the United States.
